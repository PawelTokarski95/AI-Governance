The deployment of the XGBoost credit scoring model based on a poisoned training dataset
led to a severe violation of the most critical European legal frameworks governing the
financial market and the development of artificial intelligence technologies. 

Because algorithms used for assessing creditworthiness and credit scoring of natural persons directly impact citizens
access to capital, they are officially classified as high-risk systems. Consequently, the analyzed
FinTech was subject to the strictest legal requirements, which it failed to meet at every single
stage of the project lifecycle. The primary driver for the regulator's intervention and the
imposition of massive financial penalties was the flagrant breach of the Artificial Intelligence Act (AI Act).

This regulation enforces a strict obligation on companies to implement robust data governance systems.
Under EU law, training datasets must be appropriately vetted, complete, and free of errors or statistical anomalies.

The inexperienced Data Science team completely ignored these principles, bypassing input data quality checks and data lineage tracking.
The absence of a data quality gate resulted in the uncritical implementation of a model trained on forged profiles, 
which legally constitutes a gross failure to exercise due diligence. Furthermore, the reckless inclusion of sensitive
and controversial parameters in the database (such as postal codes, gender, or the semantic analysis of private transaction titles)
led to indirect algorithmic discrimination. 

This is a direct violation of the anti-bias principles embedded in the AI Act as well
as the fundamental mandates of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). The model began unlawfully rejecting applications from
protected demographic groups, drastically violating consumer protection laws and equal treatment principles.

Concurrently, the technical infrastructure defense systems proved entirely porous, resulting in a breach of EU rules concerning the cybersecurity of AI models.
European law mandates that artificial intelligence systems must be resilient against third-party manipulation, 
explicitly requiring protection against data poisoning attacks. The fact that hackers were able to breach the internal network, secretly alter historical records, and simulate ideal purchasing profiles for straw buyers—all without triggering a single security alert—demonstrates a total absence of adversarial resilience. 

These failures triggered enforcement actions under the AI Act, leading the supervisory authority to levy the maximum possible financial penalty, calculated as a percentage of the enterprise's total annual global turnover.

Since this incident occurred within the lending sector, the consequences extended far beyond AI legislation, directly
violating the Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA), which governs the financial sector's cyber resilience. Under DORA, 
the FinTech was legally obligated to maintain modern, continuously tested ICT risk management frameworks and constantly monitor the 
integrity of its databases. The quiet, long-term manipulation of source files by external criminals, the IT department's failure to detect anomalies, 
and the lack of segregation of duties between data administrators and the development team exposed a total operational paralysis. 
This incident proved that without overarching AI Governance policies, Cybersecurity and Data Science units become isolated silos, making it legally 
and operationally impossible to run a secure financial business in Europe.
